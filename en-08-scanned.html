How to Compress a Scanned PDF Without Losing Quality (2026)

Scanned PDFs are typically 5 to 10 times larger than regular PDFs because each page is stored as a high-resolution image. This guide covers the best free methods to compress scanned PDFs while keeping the content perfectly readable.

Why Scanned PDFs Are So Large

When you scan a document, each page becomes a high-resolution image typically at 300 DPI or higher. A single A4 page scanned at 300 DPI in colour can be 1-3MB by itself. A 10-page document can easily become 20-30MB.

Best Scan Settings for Smaller Files

Optimal Scan Settings
  • Resolution: 150 DPI for text documents, 300 DPI only for photos
  • Color mode: Black and White (Grayscale) for text-only documents
  • Format: PDF directly (not TIFF or BMP then converted)
  • Mobile scanning apps: CamScanner Standard quality, Microsoft Lens, Adobe Scan Normal

What Size Reduction Can You Expect?

Typical Results for Scanned PDFs
  • 20MB colour scan at 300 DPI becomes approximately 4-6MB after Maximum compression
  • 50MB multi-page scan becomes approximately 10-20MB
  • Black and white scan at 150 DPI is already much smaller before any compression

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I make scanned text searchable after compression? ▼
ShrinkPDF focuses on compression only, not OCR text recognition. For searchable PDFs, use Adobe Acrobat or PDF24 which includes free OCR after compression.
Will signatures and stamps in my scanned document survive compression? ▼
Yes. Signatures, stamps, and all visual elements in your scanned PDF will survive compression. They may be very slightly less sharp at Maximum but remain fully visible.
What is the best compression level for scanned documents? ▼
Balanced works well for most scanned documents. Use Maximum only if the file needs to meet strict size limits such as government portal uploads.
