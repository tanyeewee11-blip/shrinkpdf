How to Compress PDF for University Submission (Free 2026 Guide)

University application portals are some of the strictest environments for PDF file sizes — and the worst time to discover your transcript is 8MB is the night before the deadline. This guide covers file size limits for Malaysian and international university portals, the best compression approach for each document type, and how to avoid the problem entirely when scanning.

File Size Limits by Portal and Document Type

University portals vary more in their limits than government portals. Some are generous (5–10MB per file), others are strict (1–2MB). Here are the common limits you'll encounter:

Typical Limits by Portal Type
  • UPU (Malaysia undergraduate applications): 2MB per document
  • UTAC (public university central system): 2MB per file
  • Individual public universities (UM, UKM, UTM, USM, UPM): 1–2MB per supporting document
  • Private university portals: Usually 2–5MB, sometimes up to 10MB
  • Postgraduate application systems: 5–10MB for thesis proposals; 2MB for supporting documents
  • International applications (UCAS UK, Common App US): 5–50MB depending on field
  • Scholarship portals (JPA, MARA, YTN): 2MB per document (see Malaysian government portal guide)

If you're unsure of the limit for your specific portal, compress to under 2MB — this fits within all Malaysian public university systems and most private ones.

Typical Limits by Document Type
  • Academic transcript: 1–2MB (these are often the largest — scanned in colour at 300 DPI)
  • SPM / STPM result slip: 1MB
  • Co-curricular certificates: 1MB each (keep individually, not merged)
  • Passport photo / IC: 1MB
  • Employer or school reference letters: 1MB
  • Personal statement (text-based): Usually under 500KB even uncompressed
  • Portfolio (design/arts): 5–10MB — check portal limit specifically

Portal-Specific Tips

UPU / MyGovUC

UPU enforces 2MB per document. The most commonly oversized files are SPM transcripts and co-curricular certificates. Scan certificates individually in black and white at 150 DPI — they typically come out under 200KB each. For official transcripts from school, re-scan rather than using the school's print-to-PDF, which often embeds images at high resolution.

Malaysian Public University Portals (UM, UKM, UTM, USM, UPM)

Each university has its own portal with slightly different limits, but 1–2MB per document is standard. These portals often allow multiple file uploads rather than requiring a single merged PDF — keep documents separate and compress each individually. Merging several compressed files into one larger upload usually hits the limit again.

Postgraduate Applications

Postgraduate portals tend to be more generous for research proposals and thesis documents (5–10MB). The strict limits apply to supporting documents — transcripts, IC, degree certificates. Proposals created in Word and exported to PDF are usually already small enough; if not, use Balanced compression to preserve formatting clarity.

UCAS (UK) and Common App (US)

These international portals are generally more generous (5MB+ per upload). Balanced compression is sufficient for most documents. The main exception is portfolios for art and design programmes, which may have specific file format and size requirements — always check the programme's specific instructions.

Compression Tips by Document Type

  • Transcripts and result slips: Use Maximum compression. These are text-heavy documents — compression has almost no visible effect on text, and you need to meet strict 1–2MB limits.
  • Certificates and awards: Use Maximum compression. Most certificates have simple designs that compress well. Verify the compressed version is still legible and any official stamps or seals are visible before uploading.
  • Reference letters: Usually under 1MB even uncompressed. If over the limit, Balanced compression is more than sufficient.
  • IC and passport copies: Use Maximum compression. Personal identity documents compress extremely well. Always verify that the IC number, name, and photo are still clearly readable after compression.
  • Portfolio PDFs: Use Balanced or Light compression to preserve image quality. Portfolios are evaluated on visual quality — don't over-compress. If the portal allows 5–10MB, use Light or Balanced only.

Step-by-Step: Compress for University Portal Upload

1

Gather All Documents and Check Their Sizes

Before compressing, list all required documents and their current file sizes. On Windows: right-click → Properties. On Mac: right-click → Get Info. This tells you which ones actually need compression — don't compress documents that are already within the limit.

2

Keep Documents Separate

Compress each document individually rather than merging them first. Most portals accept separate uploads, and individual files are easier to verify and re-compress if needed.

3

Open ShrinkPDF.fyi and Upload the First Document

No account needed. Upload your first PDF — it stays entirely on your device. Nothing is sent to any server.

4

Select the Right Compression Level

For transcripts, certificates, IC, and letters: use Maximum. For portfolios or image-heavy presentations: use Balanced. For personal statements and text documents: any level works — they're small anyway.

5

Download and Verify Each Document

After compression, open every file and check: (1) file size is within the portal limit, (2) all text is clearly readable, (3) stamps, signatures, and official seals are still visible. Do this before you start the submission — not during.

6

Keep the Originals

Save your uncompressed originals in a separate folder. Universities may request higher-quality copies later for verification, and you don't want to have only the compressed versions.

Scan Smarter — Avoid the Problem at Source

If you're scanning documents specifically for a university application, use these settings to produce files that are already within the limit before compression:

Recommended Settings for University Documents
  • Resolution: 150 DPI — sufficient for on-screen review, far smaller than 300 DPI
  • Colour mode: Black and white for text documents (transcripts, letters, ICs). Colour only for certificates with coloured official seals or for portfolios.
  • Format: PDF directly from the scanner or app — not TIFF → PDF
  • Scanning app: Microsoft Lens (free, available on iOS and Android) produces well-compressed PDFs with good clarity at default settings

A transcript scanned at 150 DPI black and white typically produces files of 50–150KB per page — well under any portal limit, with no compression required.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will the university know my PDF was compressed? ▼
No. A compressed PDF is a standard PDF — there is no flag, watermark, or metadata indicator that compression was applied. The file opens and displays identically to an uncompressed version at normal viewing sizes.
Can I compress a transcript with an official stamp or seal? ▼
Yes. Compression reduces image resolution — it does not remove or alter content. Official stamps and seals are preserved. Use Balanced compression rather than Maximum if the seal has fine detail you want to preserve clearly, but Maximum is usually fine for standard stamps.
My compressed transcript is still 3MB. What should I do? ▼
If Maximum compression still leaves you over the limit, the best fix is to re-scan at 150 DPI in black and white — this typically produces files under 100KB per page regardless of how many pages are in the transcript. Alternatively, contact the university admissions office; some portals allow exceptions or alternative submission methods.
Should I merge all my documents into one PDF before uploading? ▼
Only if the portal specifically asks for a single combined file. If you can upload separately, do so — individual files are easier to manage, compress, and verify. Merging multiple compressed files often produces a combined file that exceeds the limit again.

Yes. ShrinkPDF processes all files locally in your browser. Your IC copies, transcripts, and personal documents never leave your device and are not sent to any server. Once you close the tab, all data is cleared from browser memory.
