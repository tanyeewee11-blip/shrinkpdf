Common File Size Limits by Platform

The right compression level depends on where you're sending the file. Here are the most common limits you'll encounter:

File Size Limits by Platform Type
  • Government portals (LHDN, EPF, JPA, UPU, SPA): 1–2MB per document
  • University submission portals: 2–5MB per file
  • Job application portals (LinkedIn, Workday, etc.): 5–10MB
  • Gmail: 25MB per email total
  • Outlook.com: 20MB per email total
  • Yahoo Mail: 25MB per email total
  • Corporate email servers: Often 5–10MB — check with your IT team
  • WhatsApp / Telegram: Up to 100MB, but large files send slowly

What Actually Makes a PDF Large?

Understanding why your PDF is large helps you pick the right fix. The four main culprits are:

  • High-resolution embedded images: A scan at 300 DPI colour is the most common cause of oversized PDFs. A single scanned A4 page at 300 DPI colour can be 1–3MB on its own.
  • Uncompressed image data: Many PDF creators embed images without applying any compression. The raw pixel data is stored as-is.
  • Colour vs black and white: Colour images carry 3× more data than grayscale. For documents that don't need colour (payslips, contracts, transcripts), this is pure overhead.
  • Embedded fonts and metadata: A smaller contributor, but PDFs generated from design software or Word can carry unused font subsets and hidden metadata that add hundreds of KB.

Text itself is almost never the problem — text in PDFs is stored as vector data and compresses to almost nothing. If your PDF is mainly text and is still large, it almost certainly has embedded images or was exported without compression from another application.

Choosing the Right Compression Level

ShrinkPDF offers three compression levels. Here's when to use each:

Compression Level Guide
  • Light (20–35% reduction): Best for documents where image quality is important — product photos, design portfolios, medical imaging. Text stays identical. Use when you just need to trim a few MB.
  • Balanced (40–60% reduction): The sweet spot for most everyday documents. Ideal for email attachments, reports, and presentations. Quality is virtually indistinguishable on screen at normal zoom.
  • Maximum (65–85% reduction): Use when you have a hard size limit to hit — government portals, EPF, university submissions. Images get noticeably smaller but text remains perfectly sharp. Always verify readability after downloading.

When in doubt: use Balanced for email, Maximum for portal uploads with strict limits.

Will Compression Affect Quality?

This is the most common concern, and the answer depends on what's in your PDF:

  • Text is never affected. Text in PDFs is stored as vector data, not pixels. No matter what compression level you choose, text remains perfectly sharp and crisp.
  • Images are affected — but usually not noticeably. At Balanced compression, image quality is virtually identical on screen at normal viewing sizes. At Maximum compression, you may notice a slight reduction in sharpness if you zoom into photos at 200%+ — but for document images like charts, logos, and diagrams, it's rarely visible.
  • Formatting, colours, and structure are not affected. Compression does not change the layout, colours, or page structure of your PDF.
What compression actually does

Compression reduces embedded image resolution from 300 DPI to 150 DPI (or lower at Maximum), strips unused font subsets, and removes hidden metadata. None of these changes are visible to anyone reading the document normally.

Still Too Large After Compression?

If your PDF is still above the limit after Maximum compression, try these steps in order:

  • Re-scan at lower DPI: If the document was scanned, 300 DPI is far higher than needed for on-screen reading. Re-scan at 150 DPI in black and white — this alone reduces most scanned PDFs by 70–80% before any compression is applied. See the scanned PDF guide for details.
  • Split the document: Use the ShrinkPDF Split tool to divide a multi-page document into separate files, then compress and upload each part individually. Most portals allow multiple file uploads.
  • Remove blank or unnecessary pages: Use the Reorder tool to delete blank pages, duplicate pages, or cover pages that aren't required for the submission.
  • Compress again: Running Maximum compression a second time can sometimes squeeze out an additional 10–15% from files that have mixed content.

Specific Guide: Compressing for Email

Email has different requirements than portal uploads. Gmail and Outlook have generous limits (20–25MB), so for most documents you can use Balanced compression rather than Maximum — this preserves more image quality while still reducing the file enough for fast sending.

The exception is corporate email servers, which often have much stricter limits (5–10MB or even lower). If you're emailing to a company address and the email bounces due to size, try Maximum compression or consider an alternative.

When compression alone isn't enough for email
  • Google Drive: Upload the file and share a view link instead of attaching. Free, no size limit.
  • OneDrive / Dropbox: Same approach — share a link rather than attaching.
  • WeTransfer.com: Free for files up to 2GB, no account needed. Link expires after 7 days.

What compression level should I use for a government portal? ▼

What's the difference between Balanced and Maximum compression? ▼
Balanced reduces file size by 40–60% with virtually no visible quality change — ideal for email and everyday use. Maximum reduces by 65–85% with a slight reduction in image sharpness — use this when you have a hard size limit to meet, like a 1MB or 2MB portal cap.
