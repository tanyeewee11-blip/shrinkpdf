PDF Compression Explained — DPI, File Size and Quality

Why does a one-page PDF from Canva weigh 8MB while a 50-page Word export is only 600KB? The answer is DPI — dots per inch. Understanding how DPI and image compression interact in PDFs makes it much easier to choose the right settings and predict the result.

What Is DPI and Why Does It Matter for PDFs?

DPI stands for dots per inch — the number of pixels used to represent one inch of an image. Higher DPI means more pixels, more detail, and a larger file size. Lower DPI means fewer pixels, less detail, and a smaller file.

PDFs are a mixed format: they can contain vector elements (text, lines, shapes drawn with mathematical coordinates) and raster elements (photos, scans, embedded images stored as pixel grids). DPI only applies to the raster elements — the images.

Text has no DPI — it's always sharp

Text in a PDF is stored as font data — instructions for drawing each character. It has no DPI and scales perfectly at any size. This is why compressing a PDF never affects text quality. Only embedded images have DPI, and only images are affected by compression.

How DPI Affects File Size

The relationship between DPI and file size is roughly quadratic — double the DPI and the image takes up roughly four times the storage space:

DPIPixels in an A4 imageApprox. file size (JPEG)Use case
72 DPI595 × 842 px~50–150KBScreen viewing, email, portals
96 DPI794 × 1123 px~100–300KBScreen + light print
150 DPI1240 × 1754 px~300KB–1MBGood print quality
300 DPI2480 × 3508 px~1–4MBProfessional print / press
600 DPI4960 × 7016 px~4–16MBHigh-res scanning, archiving

A single A4 page with one photo at 300 DPI can easily be 2–4MB. A 10-page brochure with photos on every page could be 20–40MB before compression. After downsampling to 96 DPI, the same brochure might be 2–5MB — a 90% reduction.

What Compression Levels Actually Do

When you compress a PDF, the tool downsizes embedded images to a target DPI and applies JPEG compression to reduce their byte size further. Here's what ShrinkPDF's two levels do:

Balanced — targets approximately 96 DPI

Images are resampled to around 96 DPI and compressed with moderate JPEG quality. This looks sharp on any screen at 100% zoom — a monitor typically displays at 72–110 PPI, so 96 DPI provides slightly more data than the screen can show, resulting in crisp rendering. Good for documents where image quality matters: reports with charts, resumes with photos, mixed content.

Maximum — targets approximately 72 DPI

Images are resampled to around 72 DPI with aggressive JPEG compression. This matches the baseline screen resolution, so images look acceptable at 100% zoom but noticeably soft when zoomed in. Produces the smallest file. Best for: scanned documents, text-heavy files, invoices, government forms, anything where images are secondary.

Screen DPI vs Print DPI — What You Actually Need

Most people compressing PDFs are doing so to email, upload, or share digitally — not to print professionally. This changes the DPI requirement significantly:

  • Laptop screens: 96–150 PPI (pixels per inch). A 96 DPI PDF looks sharp.
  • Retina/HiDPI screens: 200–300+ PPI. A 96 DPI PDF looks acceptable; 150 DPI looks crisp.
  • Desktop office printing: 150–300 DPI is sufficient. Laser printers print at 600 DPI but most documents don't need it.
  • Professional press printing: 300 DPI minimum. Do not compress if printing commercially.

For 99% of document sharing and portal uploads, 72–96 DPI is completely fine. The recipient reads it on a screen — they don't zoom in to 400% to inspect image quality.

Frequently Asked Questions

What DPI should a PDF be for emailing? ▼
72–96 DPI is perfectly fine for emailing. The recipient will read it on a screen, and modern screens display at 72–150 PPI. A 96 DPI PDF looks sharp on any screen at normal viewing size. You only need higher DPI if the recipient will print the document professionally.
Why does my compressed PDF look blurry when I zoom in? ▼
At high zoom levels (200%+), you're seeing the individual pixels of the compressed images at 72–96 DPI — each pixel is now displayed larger than intended. This is expected and not a problem in practice — documents are read at 100% zoom, not zoomed in to inspect individual pixels. Text remains sharp at any zoom because it's vector-based.
Will compression affect a PDF I want to print? ▼
For home and office printing: no visible difference. Most office documents look fine printed at 96 DPI. For professional press or large-format printing: do not compress below 150 DPI. If the PDF will be printed at A3 or larger, keep images at 300 DPI minimum. Use Balanced compression (which targets 96 DPI) only for documents destined for screen sharing or standard A4 office printing.
Why is my Canva PDF so large? ▼
Canva exports PDFs with images at 300 DPI or higher by default, optimised for printing. A simple one-page design can be 5–15MB because every background, photo, and graphic is stored at full print resolution. Compressing to 96 DPI (Balanced) typically reduces Canva PDFs by 80–90% — a 10MB file becomes 1–2MB — with no visible quality difference on screen.
