How to Convert PDF to JPG or PNG (and Back)

Converting goes both ways depending on what you're starting with: turning PDF pages into images so you can post them, edit them, or insert them into a slide deck — or turning a stack of photos and screenshots into a single PDF for submission. JPG vs PNG — Which Should You Choose?

JPG — smaller files, the default for most uses

Use JPG for photos, scans, and documents you want to share on WhatsApp, by email, or upload to a portal with a size limit. JPG compresses the image data, which makes files significantly smaller — usually the right tradeoff unless you specifically need pixel-perfect output.

PNG — lossless, larger files

Use PNG when you need exact, lossless quality — a document with very fine text, sharp diagrams, screenshots, or anything with a transparent background. PNG doesn't lose any detail in the conversion, but the files are noticeably larger than the equivalent JPG.

If you're not sure which to pick, JPG is the right default for most real-world sharing and submission scenarios.

What Resolution (DPI) Actually Means

DPI (dots per inch) controls how many pixels are rendered per inch of the original page — higher DPI means a sharper, larger image file.

72 DPIScreen only — smallest files, fine for viewing on a phone or computer screen, not for printing
150 DPIStandard — the default for most uses: sharing, presentations, general printing
300 DPIHigh quality — for printing where fine detail matters; files are considerably larger

One thing DPI can't fix: if the source PDF itself was already low-resolution (a blurry scan, for instance), converting at 300 DPI won't add detail that wasn't in the original — you'll just get a larger file of the same blurry image.

A note on WEBP and transparency

JPG and PNG images are embedded at their original resolution with no quality loss. PNG transparency carries over into the PDF correctly. WEBP is the one exception: it's converted to JPEG internally before being embedded, so a transparent background in a WEBP file will not stay transparent in the output — if transparency matters, convert that image to PNG first.

Common Conversion Scenarios

Posting a document page on social media

Some platforms don't accept PDF uploads directly — convert the relevant page to JPG first.

Inserting a PDF page into a slide deck or document

PowerPoint, Google Slides, and Word all accept images directly, but not PDFs. Convert the page you need to PNG for the sharpest result if it contains text or diagrams.

Submitting a scanned form as a single PDF

If you photographed several pages of a form with your phone, convert all the photos into one PDF instead of sending separate image files — most portals expect a single PDF, not a folder of JPGs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I convert my PDF to JPG or PNG? ▼
Use JPG for most everyday purposes — photos, scans, and documents you want to share on WhatsApp or by email. JPG files are significantly smaller. Use PNG when you need lossless quality, for example a document with very fine text, sharp lines, or a transparent background that needs to stay pixel-perfect.
What DPI should I choose when converting PDF to images? ▼
72 DPI is enough for on-screen viewing only. 150 DPI is the standard choice for most uses — sharing, presentations, and general printing. 300 DPI is for high-quality printing where fine detail matters; the files will be noticeably larger.
Will converting images to PDF lose quality? ▼
JPG and PNG images are embedded into the PDF at their original resolution with no recompression, so there's no quality loss in that step. If you separately compress the resulting PDF afterward, that compression step does reduce image quality depending on the level chosen.
Can I mix JPG, PNG, and WEBP images in one PDF? ▼
Yes. You can combine different image formats in the same conversion. One detail worth knowing: WEBP images are converted to JPEG during the process before being embedded, so a transparent background in a WEBP file will not stay transparent in the output PDF. PNG transparency is preserved.
Is there a page or file limit for either conversion? ▼
No. Both tools work on PDFs and image sets of any size, since everything runs locally in your browser rather than on a server with storage limits. Larger files simply take longer to process depending on your device.
Is it safe to convert documents with personal information? ▼
Yes. ShrinkPDF processes everything locally in your browser — neither your PDF nor your images are ever uploaded to a server, in either conversion direction.
