How to Sign a PDF for Free — What Counts as Legal

"Sign a PDF" means two different things depending on what you're signing. Putting your signature on a school permission slip and signing an employment contract don't need the same tool, even though both technically involve "signing a PDF." Here's the distinction, and free options for both.

Two Different Things Called "Signing a PDF"

Signature image overlay

Drawing your signature with a mouse or finger, typing your name in a script font, or pasting a photo of your handwritten signature onto a PDF page. This places a picture on the document. It looks like a signature, but there's no record of who placed it, when, or from where — it can be copied off the page and reused just as easily as any other image.

Legally binding electronic signature

A structured process from a dedicated e-signature service that captures the signer's identity, timestamp, and an audit trail alongside the signature itself, meeting requirements like the U.S. ESIGN Act or the EU's eIDAS regulation. This is what actually holds up if a signed document is ever disputed.

Most free "sign a PDF" tools — including the ones built into your browser — only do the first one. That's not a limitation to work around for most everyday paperwork; it's simply the wrong tool for a contract.

Free Options for a Quick Signature

Firefox has a built-in signature tool

Open the PDF in Firefox, click Add signature in the toolbar, then draw, type, or upload an image of your signature. Place it on the page and save. No account, no upload to any third-party service — it happens entirely inside the browser's own PDF viewer.

Chrome and Edge only offer basic drawing

Neither browser has a dedicated signature feature. You can open a PDF and use the built-in drawing/annotation tool to sketch a signature freehand, which works but is clumsier than Firefox's purpose-built tool, especially with a mouse instead of a touchscreen.

Any of these are fine for forms, permission slips, informal sign-offs, or acknowledging you've read something — situations where a visual signature is all that's actually expected.

When You Need a Real, Legally Binding Signature

For contracts, NDAs, employment offers, lease agreements, or anything where you might later need to prove a specific person signed on a specific date, use a dedicated e-signature service rather than a browser drawing tool. PDF editor suites like UPDF and PDF Extra include signature and form-filling tools alongside editing and conversion, with a one-time license option instead of a recurring subscription — worth a look if you're already comparing PDF editors for other reasons.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is drawing my signature on a PDF the same as an electronic signature? ▼
Not legally. Drawing, typing, or pasting an image of your signature onto a PDF places a picture on the page — it's fine for informal approvals but carries no audit trail proving who signed, when, or from where. A legally binding electronic signature comes from a dedicated e-signature service that records that information alongside the signature.
Does any browser have a built-in way to sign a PDF for free? ▼
Yes. Firefox has a native signature tool in its PDF viewer — draw, type, or upload an image of your signature and place it on the page, no account needed. Chrome and Edge only offer a basic draw/annotate tool, not a dedicated signature feature.
When do I actually need a legally binding e-signature instead of just an image? ▼
For contracts, NDAs, employment offers, and anything where you might need to prove later that a specific person signed on a specific date, use a dedicated e-signature service. For internal approvals, quick sign-offs, or forms where a visual signature is enough, a plain image overlay is fine.
