How to Send a Large PDF by Email

Email providers have attachment size limits — and PDF files from scans, Canva exports, or presentations can easily exceed them. This guide covers the limits for every major provider, how to compress your PDF to fit, and what to do when even a compressed file is still too large to attach directly.

Even if your Gmail allows 25MB outgoing, the recipient's mail server may only accept 10MB. Corporate and government email servers commonly have lower limits. For important emails, aim for under 10MB to ensure reliable delivery.

Step 1 — Compress the PDF First

Before trying any workaround, compress the PDF. Most PDFs compress to 20–80% of their original size, which is often enough to fit within email limits.

3

Check the New File Size

Right-click the downloaded file → Properties (Windows) or Get Info (Mac) to see the new size. If it's now under 10MB, attach it to your email and send normally.

Step 2 — Still Too Large? Share a Link Instead

If the PDF is still too large after compression — typically large portfolios, multi-chapter reports, or high-resolution brochures — share it via a cloud link instead of attaching it directly.

Google Drive (recommended for Gmail users)

  1. Upload the PDF to Google Drive (drive.google.com)
  2. Right-click the file → Share → Get link
  3. Set access to Anyone with the link
  4. Copy the link and paste it into your email body

Gmail even detects large attachments automatically and offers to upload to Drive and insert a link — you'll see a prompt when you try to attach a file over 25MB.

Outlook OneDrive (for Outlook users)

  1. When attaching in Outlook, click the paperclip → Browse cloud locations → OneDrive
  2. Upload the PDF to OneDrive first, then select it
  3. Outlook inserts a sharing link automatically

Dropbox / WeTransfer

Upload to Dropbox and share a link, or use WeTransfer (wetransfer.com) for a simple link-based transfer without needing a cloud account. WeTransfer free tier allows up to 2GB per transfer.

Step 3 — Split Into Parts

If the recipient specifically needs the PDF as a file attachment (not a link), and it's still too large after compression, split it into smaller parts using ShrinkPDF's Split tool. Send each part as a separate email with clear labelling (e.g. "Report Q1 2026 — Part 1 of 3").

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Gmail's PDF attachment size limit? ▼
Gmail allows attachments up to 25MB. However, the recipient's mail server may have a lower limit — many corporate and government servers only accept 10MB or less. For reliable delivery to any recipient, compress your PDF to under 10MB. Gmail will automatically offer to insert a Google Drive link if your attachment exceeds 25MB.
Why does Gmail say my PDF is too large when it's under 25MB? ▼
Email attachments are encoded in base64 when transmitted, which adds approximately 33% to the file size. A 20MB PDF becomes roughly 27MB when encoded — exceeding Gmail's 25MB limit. The practical limit for a single Gmail attachment is around 18–19MB. Compress to under 15MB for reliable sending.
Can I send a 50MB PDF by email? ▼
Not directly as an attachment — all major email providers have limits below 50MB. Compress it first: a 50MB PDF typically compresses to 5–15MB with ShrinkPDF. If still over 25MB after compression, upload to Google Drive or Dropbox and share the link in your email instead.
Does compressing a PDF affect how it looks in email? ▼
Visually, no — at Light or Balanced, the recipient won't notice a difference when opening the attachment normally. Worth knowing: ShrinkPDF re-renders each page as an image to compress it, so the compressed file loses real selectable/searchable text and any hyperlinks, even though it displays the same. If the recipient needs to search or click links in the document, send the original alongside the compressed copy.
