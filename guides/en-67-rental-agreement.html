Two Types of Signatures — Only One Survives Compression

This is the single most important thing to understand before compressing a tenancy agreement:

Signature TypeWhat It Looks LikeAfter Compression
Wet-ink signature (scanned)Handwritten signature on paper, then scanned or photographed✅ Visually fine. It's just part of the image — compresses normally with no issue.
Digital signature (cryptographic)Created via DocuSign, Adobe Sign, or similar — has a verification seal/certificate❌ Invalidated. The cryptographic seal is destroyed when the page is re-rendered as an image.
⚠️ How to tell which type you have

Open the PDF and look at the signature area. If you see a "Signed by [Name] — Certificate verified" banner, a blue ribbon icon, or a signature panel with a digital certificate, it's a cryptographic signature — do not compress this copy if you need to preserve verification. If the signature is simply a handwritten scrawl on the page with no verification banner, it's a wet-ink signature scanned into the PDF — completely safe to compress.

When Compression Is Completely Safe

  • The agreement was printed, signed by hand, and scanned/photographed. This is the most common case for rental agreements in Malaysia and most of Southeast Asia. The signature is just pixels in the image — compression doesn't change its meaning.
  • You're sending it for someone to read, not to digitally verify. A landlord checking your previous tenancy, a bank assessing your rental history, or an employer requesting proof of address — these all just need a legible copy, not a cryptographically valid one.
  • You're keeping the original separately. Compress a copy for sharing, keep the original (or a high-quality scan) for your own records or for situations requiring the uncompressed version.

When to Be Careful

  • The agreement has a digital signature certificate. If it was signed via DocuSign, Adobe Sign, or a similar e-signature platform, compressing it will strip the verification. If the recipient needs to verify the signature is authentic, send the original file unmodified.
  • The agreement has an official stamp or seal that needs to remain crisp. Some agreements have a notary stamp or company seal. At Balanced compression these usually remain legible; at Maximum compression very fine stamp details can soften slightly. Use Balanced rather than Maximum for documents with official stamps.
  • You're submitting to a government authority that explicitly requires verification. If a portal specifically states it needs a digitally verifiable document (rare for tenancy agreements, but possible for some formal legal processes), don't compress — submit the original.

FAQ

Is it safe to compress a signed tenancy agreement? ▼
It depends on the signature type. If it's a handwritten signature that was scanned (the common case for printed-and-signed agreements), compression is completely safe — the signature is just part of the image. If it's a digital signature created through DocuSign or Adobe Sign with a verification certificate, compression will invalidate that certificate. Check the signature area for a verification banner before deciding.
Will the rental amount and terms still be legible after compression? ▼
Yes, at Balanced compression. Balanced preserves text clarity well enough that rental amounts, dates, and clause text remain clearly readable. We recommend always doing a quick visual check after compressing — zoom to 100% and confirm the key figures (rent, deposit, dates) are sharp before submitting to a bank or government portal.
Can I compress a tenancy agreement before submitting it for a visa application? ▼
Yes, in almost all cases. Visa applications generally need proof of accommodation that's legible, not a cryptographically verified document. Compress with Balanced or Maximum to meet the typical 2MB visa portal limit, and verify the address, names, and dates are clearly readable before submitting.
My agreement has a company stamp. Will it still be recognisable after compression? ▼
Yes, at Balanced compression. Official stamps are usually large and simple enough that Balanced compression doesn't blur them significantly. If the stamp has very fine detail (small text inside the stamp), avoid Maximum compression, which can soften fine details more noticeably.
