Compress PDF for LinkedIn, Portfolio & Job Application Upload

Your CV is often the first thing a recruiter sees — a bloated, slow-to-open PDF creates a bad first impression before they've read a word. LinkedIn has a 5MB limit for profile featured files. ATS systems like Workday, Greenhouse, and Taleo enforce limits ranging from 5–10MB. This guide covers the right compression approach for each document type in a job search context.

File Size Limits by Platform

Job search platform file size limits (2026)
  • LinkedIn Featured section (profile): 5MB per file
  • LinkedIn Easy Apply (job applications): 5MB per file
  • Indeed resume upload: 5MB
  • Workday (ATS): 10MB per file
  • Greenhouse (ATS): 10MB per file
  • Lever (ATS): 10MB per file
  • Taleo (Oracle, ATS): 5–10MB depending on company configuration
  • JobStreet: 5MB
  • Jobstreet by SEEK: 5MB
  • Email attachment to recruiter: Typically 10–25MB (depends on recipient's email server)

A well-formatted CV should be under 500KB — even with a professional headshot. If your CV is larger than 1MB, something is wrong: unnecessary embedded images, a scanned PDF instead of a digital one, or fonts that haven't been subset. A portfolio with design work is legitimately larger, but should still be under 5MB for most platforms.

Compressing Your CV / Resume

A digital CV — created in Word, Google Docs, Canva, or a similar tool — should be tiny. Here's why CVs sometimes become unexpectedly large and how to fix it:

  • Scanned CV: If your CV was scanned from a printed copy, it's stored as images and can be 3–8MB. Use Maximum compression — this reduces it significantly while keeping text readable. Better solution: recreate it digitally in Word or Google Docs and export as PDF.
  • Canva or design tool export: Canva exports at 300 DPI print quality by default. A heavily designed CV can be 5–15MB. Use Balanced compression — this typically brings it to under 1MB without affecting the visual design.
  • Word to PDF with embedded images: CVs with a professional photo embedded at high resolution can be 3–5MB. Use Balanced compression.
  • Google Docs export: Usually generates small files (under 300KB). Rarely needs compression.
Recommended compression level for CVs

Use Balanced for most CVs. This reduces file size by 40–60% while keeping text sharp and your headshot looking professional. Use Maximum only if Balanced doesn't get you under your target size — it reduces photo quality slightly but keeps text completely sharp.

Compressing a Portfolio PDF

Portfolio PDFs — for designers, photographers, architects, illustrators — require more care than CVs. The work itself is being evaluated, so image quality is the priority, not maximum compression.

  • Use Light compression: This reduces file size by 20–35% while preserving the highest image quality. For a 20MB portfolio, Light compression typically brings it to 12–15MB — under LinkedIn's 5MB limit if the original was already well-optimised.
  • If Light isn't enough, use Balanced: Balanced is usually acceptable for portfolios — images look sharp at normal viewing sizes. At 200%+ zoom, you may notice softness, but recruiters and clients don't zoom in to inspect pixel sharpness.
  • Avoid Maximum for portfolios: Maximum compression significantly degrades image quality, which defeats the purpose of a portfolio. Only use Maximum if the platform absolutely requires it and the alternative is not being able to submit at all.
  • Consider splitting the portfolio: If your portfolio has 50 pages and must stay under 5MB, consider splitting it into a highlights version (10–15 best pieces) and a full version (shared via link). Recruiters rarely review more than the first 10 pages anyway.

Uploading to LinkedIn

LinkedIn allows PDFs in two places: your Featured section (profile page) and Easy Apply (job applications). Both have a 5MB limit.

Adding to LinkedIn Featured section

  1. Compress your PDF to under 5MB (under 2MB is ideal for fast loading)
  2. Go to your LinkedIn profile and click the Add profile section button
  3. Under "Recommended", select Featured
  4. Choose Add a media and upload your PDF
  5. Add a title and description — these appear alongside the file and help recruiters understand what they're clicking

Tip: LinkedIn renders a preview of the first page of your PDF. Make sure your first page looks polished — it's the thumbnail that appears on your profile.

Using Easy Apply with a PDF

  1. Click Easy Apply on a job listing
  2. At the resume/CV upload step, click Upload and select your compressed PDF
  3. LinkedIn may also parse your CV and pre-fill application fields — a clean, text-based PDF (not a scan) parses much better than a scanned or image-heavy CV

ATS and Job Application Portals

Applicant Tracking Systems (Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, Taleo, iCIMS) are the backend systems companies use to manage applications. They have their own quirks beyond file size:

  • Parsability matters more than aesthetics: ATS systems extract text from your CV to populate candidate profiles. Scanned CVs, heavily designed CVs with text in images, and CVs in non-standard fonts parse poorly. A clean, text-based PDF exports best from Word or Google Docs.
  • Standard PDF is safest: Some ATS systems have trouble with PDF/A format or PDFs with complex transparency and layers from design tools. If your CV is rejected for "format not supported," try exporting from Word as a standard PDF rather than from Canva.
  • Don't over-compress: ATS systems sometimes copy text from PDFs for candidate records. Over-compressed PDFs (especially scans at Maximum compression) may have reduced text accuracy if OCR is involved. For clean digital PDFs, this is not an issue — text is always perfectly preserved regardless of compression level.

Maintaining Quality for Professional Documents

The professional context of job applications means quality matters more than it might for a utility document submission. Here's what to check before submitting:

  • Open the compressed PDF at 100% zoom and read through it. Text should be sharp and clear. Headshot (if any) should look professional — not pixelated or artifacted.
  • Check colours if your CV uses brand colours or a designed template. Compression should preserve colour values exactly — if something looks off, re-export from the original source at a slightly higher quality setting.
  • Don't rely on hyperlinks working if your CV contains links (LinkedIn profile, portfolio URL, GitHub) — ShrinkPDF's compression flattens each page into an image, so links are not clickable in the compressed output. Keep the original PDF on hand to send if a recruiter needs the links to work.
  • Check the file name before uploading. "Compressed_CV_v3_FINAL2.pdf" looks unprofessional. Rename it to "FirstName_LastName_CV.pdf" before submitting.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the file size limit for LinkedIn PDF uploads? ▼
LinkedIn's limit is 5MB per file for both Featured section uploads and Easy Apply applications. A well-formatted digital CV should be well under 1MB. If yours is larger, it likely contains a high-resolution headshot or was exported from a design tool at print quality — Balanced compression will bring it within limits.
Will compression affect how my CV is parsed by ATS systems? ▼
It can, and this is worth knowing before you compress. ShrinkPDF re-renders each page as an image, so the compressed file no longer has a real text layer for an ATS to extract from — the same situation as a scanned CV, which many basic ATS systems struggle to parse or reject outright. If your CV is small enough to submit uncompressed (most text-based CVs are well under 1MB), submit the original. If you must compress to meet a strict size limit, check the portal's stated requirements, since some explicitly ask for a "text-based" or "non-scanned" PDF.
Should I compress my portfolio to Maximum quality for LinkedIn? ▼
No — use Light or Balanced for portfolios. Your work is being evaluated on visual quality, and Maximum compression noticeably degrades images at closer inspection. If Light compression doesn't get you under 5MB, use Balanced. If Balanced isn't enough, consider creating a highlights version of your portfolio with fewer but higher-quality pieces.
Does LinkedIn compress my PDF again after I upload it? ▼
LinkedIn may re-process uploaded PDFs for display purposes (generating previews, thumbnails), but the original file you uploaded is stored and available for download by recruiters and connections. Start with a well-compressed file so both the preview and the download look professional.
My CV is 15MB because I have a Canva template. What should I do? ▼
Canva exports at 300 DPI print quality, which makes design-heavy CVs very large. Use Balanced compression — this typically reduces a Canva CV from 10–15MB to under 1MB with no visible change at normal screen zoom. Your design, layout, colours, and fonts are all preserved.
Will the hyperlinks in my CV still work after compression? ▼
No. ShrinkPDF's compression re-renders each page as an image, so clickable hyperlinks are not preserved in the compressed file, even though they look the same visually. If a recruiter needs to click through to your LinkedIn, portfolio, or GitHub, send the original uncompressed PDF for the links, and only use the compressed version where a portal has a strict file-size limit and visual reading is enough.
