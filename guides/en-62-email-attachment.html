How to Compress a PDF for Email

1

Check your current file size

Before compressing, check the PDF's current size. On Windows: right-click → Properties. On Mac: right-click → Get Info. On mobile: long-press the file. This tells you how much reduction you actually need.

2

Open ShrinkPDF

Go to shrinkpdf.fyi in your browser. Works on any device — phone, tablet, laptop. Your PDF is processed locally and never uploaded to any server.

3

Select the right compression level

See the section below for which level to choose based on your email provider and document type.

4

Download and send

After compression, check the result size shown on screen. If it's under your email provider's limit, download and attach to your email. The entire process typically takes under a minute.

✅ Safe target: stay under 10MB

Even though Gmail allows 25MB, the recipient's email server may have a lower limit — especially corporate and government accounts. As a rule, keep email PDF attachments under 10MB to reliably clear any recipient's inbox. Under 5MB is better for very formal or official submissions.

When Compression Isn't Enough

If your PDF is still too large after Maximum compression, here are the alternatives:

  • Upload to Google Drive and share a link. Gmail integrates Drive — you can insert a Drive link directly from the compose window. The recipient gets access without any size limitation.
  • Split the document. If you're sending a multi-page report, use the Split PDF tool to send only the pages the recipient needs.
  • Use WeTransfer or Dropbox. Both allow you to share a download link for free. WeTransfer allows up to 2GB per transfer on the free plan.
  • Ask to receive the document in person or via USB. For very large technical documents, sometimes a physical handoff is the right answer.

FAQ

Gmail said it converted my PDF to a Drive link. Is that okay? ▼
Yes — Gmail automatically does this when the attachment exceeds 25MB. The recipient gets a Drive link instead of a direct attachment. This works fine for most purposes, but some formal submissions (visa applications, government forms) specifically require a file attachment rather than a link. In those cases, compress the PDF to under 25MB and re-send.
My email bounced with "message too large". What does that mean exactly? ▼
The receiving mail server rejected your email because the total message size exceeded its limit. The limit is set by the recipient's mail server administrator, not Gmail or Outlook. Corporate and government mail servers often have lower limits (10–20MB) than consumer email. Compress the PDF to under 10MB and try again — if it still bounces, the limit may be even lower, or there may be a file type restriction.
Will the recipient be able to read the compressed PDF normally? ▼
Yes. The compressed PDF opens in any PDF reader (Adobe Acrobat, Foxit, browser-based viewers, Preview on Mac) exactly as before. The only change is that text is no longer selectable or searchable in the compressed version — it has been re-rendered as an image. For most email use cases (reading, printing, filing), this makes no practical difference.
