Compress PDF for Printing — What DPI You Actually Need

The question isn't whether to compress before printing — it's whether your print use case requires the high resolution that compression removes. For most office printing, compression is completely safe. For professional print production, it isn't. Here's exactly where the line is.

When It's Safe to Compress Before Printing

Safe to compress with Balanced
  • Office documents: Reports, contracts, letters, and forms printed on a standard laser or inkjet printer. Text prints perfectly; image quality is acceptable at A4 size.
  • Presentation handouts: Slides printed as A4 handouts. Images look fine at normal print size — softness only becomes visible when the printed page is viewed very close up.
  • Scanned documents for archiving: Compressing before printing a reference copy is fine — text remains fully legible.
  • Invoices, receipts, certificates: Text-heavy, so compression has no visible impact on print quality.

When NOT to Compress Before Printing

Do not compress if you're printing professionally
  • Brochures and marketing materials: Images at 96 DPI will print noticeably softer than the 300 DPI standard for commercial print.
  • Photo books or portfolios: Photo quality is the point — compression defeats the purpose.
  • Business cards: Small size means every pixel matters — needs 300+ DPI.
  • Large format prints (A1, A0, banners): Actually fine at lower DPI because they're viewed from a distance, but if the file already contains high-resolution imagery, there's no benefit to compressing before sending to a print shop.

Text vs Images in Print

This is the key distinction that determines whether compression matters for your print job:

  • In the original PDF, text is vector-based: it prints at the maximum resolution of your printer, regardless of any DPI setting, because there's no pixel grid to begin with.
  • ShrinkPDF's compression re-renders every page as an image — including text pages. Once compressed, what used to be vector text becomes a raster image at whatever DPI that level targets (as low as ~72 DPI at Maximum). On screen this is hard to notice; printed, low-DPI raster text can look visibly softer or fuzzier than the crisp vector text in the original, especially on smaller fonts.
  • Images in PDFs are raster-based already, so the same logic applies more straightforwardly: at 96 DPI printed at A4, each pixel covers about 1/96 inch, visibly softer than a 300 DPI original.

Practical conclusion: for anything you intend to print, including text-heavy documents (reports, contracts, letters), use Light compression or skip compression entirely if quality matters — don't assume text "doesn't count" toward print quality, because after compression it's no longer real vector text. For image-heavy documents (brochures, photo layouts), use Balanced compression only if you're printing at home or in an office — not for professional print production.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I compress a PDF and then print it without quality loss? ▼
For text documents: yes, no visible quality loss. For image-heavy documents printed at home or office: Balanced compression is acceptable — images look fine at A4 printing size. For professional print production: no — keep the original high-resolution file for print and use the compressed version only for digital sharing.
What DPI should a PDF be for printing on a home printer? ▼
Home inkjet printers typically print at 600–1200 DPI for the printer mechanism, but image content in PDFs only needs to be 96–150 DPI for acceptable print quality at A4 size. Balanced compression targets 96 DPI, which is sufficient for home printing. Maximum (72 DPI) is acceptable for text-only documents but may show slight softness on printed photos.
I need to email a PDF to a print shop. Should I compress it? ▼
No. Print shops need high-resolution files (300+ DPI) to produce professional-quality output. Do not compress a PDF you're sending for professional printing. If the file is too large to email, use a Google Drive or WeTransfer link instead of compressing the file.
