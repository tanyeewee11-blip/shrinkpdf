What Needs to Stay Sharp

Medical documents differ from typical paperwork because specific details — lab values, dosages, dates, diagnosis codes — must remain unambiguous. The compression itself doesn't alter content, but overly aggressive compression on a poor-quality scan can make small numbers harder to read.

ElementRisk LevelRecommendation
Typed text (diagnosis, summary)Low riskStays clear even at Maximum compression
Lab result tables (numbers, units)Medium riskUse Balanced to keep small numbers crisp
Handwritten notesMedium-high riskUse Light or Balanced — handwriting is harder to compress without softening
X-ray / scan images embedded in reportHigh risk for diagnosis, low for submissionUse Light if image clarity matters; Balanced is fine for administrative submission only
Doctor's signature/stampLow riskStays recognisable at any compression level
⚠️ For diagnostic use, don't compress imaging

If your PDF contains an X-ray, MRI, CT scan, or ultrasound image that a doctor needs to review for diagnostic purposes, do not compress it. Medical imaging requires full resolution for accurate clinical assessment. Compression is appropriate for administrative submission (insurance, SOCSO, employer records) where the report is being read for information, not diagnostic interpretation.

How to Compress Safely

1

Identify what the document contains

Check whether your report is mostly typed text, includes lab tables, has handwritten notes, or contains embedded medical imaging. This determines which compression level to use.

2

Open ShrinkPDF

Go to shrinkpdf.fyi. Your medical document is processed entirely in your browser and is never uploaded to any server — this matters for health information privacy.

3

Choose Balanced compression

Balanced is the right default for medical documents — it reduces file size by 40–60% while keeping lab values, dates, and diagnosis text clearly legible. Reserve Maximum for purely administrative documents (cover letters, referral slips) with no fine numerical detail.

4

Zoom in and verify all numbers are readable

This is the most important step for medical documents. Zoom to 150–200% and check every lab value, date, and dosage number. If anything looks ambiguous (is that a 3 or an 8?), reduce to Light compression and try again.

FAQ

Will compressing my medical report affect my insurance claim? ▼
No, as long as the compressed document remains clearly legible. Insurance assessors need to read the report's content — diagnosis, dates, treatment details — they don't need a pixel-perfect original. Use Balanced compression and verify all numbers and text are sharp before submitting. If you're concerned, keep the original file as a backup in case the insurer requests additional clarity.
Can I compress an X-ray or scan image embedded in a PDF report? ▼
For administrative submission (insurance, SOCSO claims where the imaging is supporting evidence rather than the primary diagnostic tool), Balanced compression is acceptable. For situations where a doctor needs to clinically review the image itself, do not compress — submit the original at full resolution. When in doubt, ask the recipient whether they need diagnostic-quality imaging or just a legible record.
SOCSO rejected my upload for being too large. What level should I use? ▼
SOCSO's portal typically limits uploads to 2MB per file. Use Maximum compression for medical certificates and typed reports — they'll typically compress to under 500KB. For scanned reports with lab tables, use Balanced first; if still over 2MB, step up to Maximum and verify the numbers remain readable at 150% zoom before resubmitting.
