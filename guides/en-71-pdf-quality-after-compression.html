What Does a Compressed PDF Look Like?
Honest Quality Guide

Before you compress an important document, you want to know: will it look bad? Will the text be blurry? Will photos become pixelated? The answer depends on your compression level and document type — and it's more reassuring than you might expect for most real-world use cases.

How Compression Changes Your PDF

ShrinkPDF compresses by re-rendering each page as a JPEG image at a lower resolution, then assembling those images into a new PDF. This means:

  • The visual appearance is preserved — colours, layout, photos, and design elements all look the same at normal viewing size
  • Text is no longer selectable or searchable — it becomes part of the image layer
  • Resolution is reduced — from typically 150–300 DPI down to 72–120 DPI depending on compression level
  • File size drops dramatically — especially for image-heavy PDFs

The key insight: screen resolution is 72–96 DPI. Your original PDF at 144–300 DPI contains far more pixel data than any screen can display. Compressing down to 96–120 DPI produces a file that is visually identical on screen — the "lost" data was never visible anyway.

✅ The key insight about screen viewing

Your computer monitor displays at 72–96 DPI. Your phone screen at 150–400 DPI but scales content down. At normal 100% zoom, a document at 72 DPI looks completely identical to one at 300 DPI — there's simply no extra resolution for the screen to show. The difference only appears when you zoom in well past 100%.

When Quality Actually Matters

Quality matters for:

  • Print production — sending to a printer for physical printing. Compression reduces DPI below print-quality threshold. Do not compress print-ready files.
  • Medical imaging diagnosis — X-rays, MRIs, and scans need full resolution for clinical assessment.
  • Archiving original documents — keep the original uncompressed copy alongside any compressed versions.

Quality does NOT matter for:

  • Portal submissions (LHDN, UPU, MyEG, university) — reviewers read documents at screen resolution. Balanced or Maximum compression is indistinguishable.
  • Email attachments — recipients view on screen. Light or Balanced is perfect.
  • WhatsApp sharing — WhatsApp itself re-compresses images. Maximum compression is fine.
  • Job applications — recruiters view resumes on screen. Balanced compression is not noticeable.
  • Bank / insurance submissions — numbers and signatures need to be legible, not print-sharp. Balanced works.

How to Check Quality Before Submitting

1

Open the compressed PDF

After downloading, open the compressed file in any PDF viewer (browser, Adobe Reader, Preview).

2

View at 100% zoom

Set zoom to 100% (Ctrl+1 on most viewers). This is how the recipient will view the document. If it looks good here, it will look good to them.

3

Check the critical content

Read through names, dates, numbers, and key text. For ICs and passports, check the ID number. For payslips, check salary figures. For contracts, check the clause text.

4

If not satisfied, use a lighter compression level

Go back to ShrinkPDF, select a lower compression level, and compress again. Light compression almost never produces visible quality loss.

FAQ

Will the recipient know my PDF was compressed? ▼
At Balanced compression, almost certainly not. The visual appearance at normal screen viewing is identical to the original. The file will open in any PDF viewer normally. The only way to detect compression is to check the document's metadata or zoom in significantly beyond 100%.
My original PDF has very small text (8pt or smaller). Will compression affect it? ▼
At Light and Balanced, very small text (8pt and below) remains legible at 100% zoom for most documents. At Maximum, there can be slight softness on very small text when zoomed in past 150%. If your document has critical fine print (legal disclaimers, footnotes), use Balanced rather than Maximum, and verify the small text is clearly readable at 100% after compressing.
Does compression affect PDF colours? ▼
JPEG compression can introduce very subtle colour shifts in highly saturated or precisely specified colours (like Pantone brand colours). At Balanced compression, these shifts are generally imperceptible. At Maximum, very vibrant colours (deep reds, electric blues) can look very slightly muted. For brand-critical documents, use Light or Balanced compression and compare the result against the original.
